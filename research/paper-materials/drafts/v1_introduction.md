## I. Introduction

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is a prevalent and economically burdensome mental health condition characterized by persistent, uncontrollable worry and associated somatic symptoms. Epidemiological surveys estimate a lifetime prevalence of approximately 7.6% in the general population [1], and annual societal costs in the United States alone exceed US$42 billion when direct healthcare expenditure, productivity loss, and comorbid-condition burden are combined [2]. Despite this impact, GAD remains severely under-detected: large-scale cross-national surveys indicate that fewer than 10% of affected individuals receive minimally adequate care within the first year of onset [3]. Timely identification is therefore a critical clinical priority, yet current practice relies predominantly on self-report instruments such as the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale (GAD-7), which are susceptible to recall bias, social desirability effects, and cross-cultural variability [4]. The lack of objective, physiologically grounded biomarkers represents a fundamental barrier to improving detection rates and monitoring treatment response.

Advances in functional neuroimaging have linked GAD to hypo-activation of the prefrontal cortex (PFC) during cognitively demanding tasks, including inhibitory control and working memory paradigms [5]. These hemodynamic signatures suggest that non-invasive cortical oximetry over the PFC could serve as a candidate objective biomarker for GAD. Functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) is particularly well-suited for this purpose. By quantifying changes in oxygenated (HbO), deoxygenated (HbR), and total (HbT) hemoglobin concentration through the modified Beer-Lambert law, fNIRS provides spatially resolved measurement of cortical activation at sampling rates up to 10 Hz [6]. Unlike functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), fNIRS requires no magnetic shielding, accommodates participant movement, and is readily deployable outside specialized research facilities. Relative to electroencephalography (EEG), fNIRS offers superior spatial localization with less susceptibility to scalp electromyographic artifacts, as detailed in continuous-wave fNIRS instrumentation reviews [7], albeit without the millisecond temporal precision of electrophysiological methods. These characteristics position fNIRS as a practical platform for translational psychiatric biomarker research.

Machine learning has been applied to fNIRS signals for classifying anxiety-related mental states, with methods ranging from support vector machines to shallow neural networks trained on hand-crafted spectral and temporal features extracted from individual channels [8], [9]. Although promising classification accuracies have been reported, these approaches share a common limitation: they treat fNIRS channels as interchangeable scalar time series, discarding the inherent anatomical spatial structure of the optode array. Optodes placed over the PFC according to the international 10-20 system occupy fixed scalp positions with predictable cortical projections, such that adjacent channels share partially overlapping fields of view and their hemodynamic activations reflect the organization of underlying neural networks. Collapsing channels into unordered feature vectors eliminates this topology, preventing models from learning spatially coherent co-activation patterns that may be diagnostically informative. Moreover, no prior study has applied end-to-end spatiotemporal deep learning directly to fNIRS volume data for GAD classification. The representational capacity of modern transformer architectures — demonstrated extensively in video understanding [10], [11] — thus remains largely unexplored in this clinical context.

This paper addresses these limitations by proposing a grid-based spatiotemporal encoding pipeline that transforms fNIRS channel time series into video-like tensors amenable to a 3D Vision Transformer (3D ViT). Signals from 23 prefrontal fNIRS channels are mapped onto a 5×7 spatial grid according to their optode coordinates under the 10-20 system. Empty grid positions, arising from the sparse placement of 23 channels within the 35-cell grid, are filled via Gaussian radial basis function (RBF) interpolation to produce spatially continuous hemodynamic frames. Stacking T consecutive frames yields a (T, H, W) volume tensor that, when replicated across three input channels, constitutes a (3, T, H, W) clip compatible with spatiotemporal patch tokenization following the Video Vision Transformer (ViViT) architecture [11]. The 3D ViT jointly attends across spatial and temporal patch dimensions, enabling the model to exploit both the anatomical co-activation structure of the PFC network and the temporally evolving hemodynamic response without manual feature engineering. We evaluate this framework across four cognitive paradigms — Go/No-Go (GNG), Serial Subtraction (SS), 1-Back Working Memory, and Verbal Fluency — using leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) cross-validation to provide unbiased estimates of subject-level generalization. We further provide statistical grounding for the use of HbT as the primary hemodynamic measure, demonstrating that HbT uniquely achieves significant group discrimination at the most responsive prefrontal channel.

The principal contributions of this work are: (1) a novel 1D-to-2D grid encoding scheme that preserves the anatomical spatial topology of fNIRS optodes and enables spatially structured input to deep networks; (2) a 3D video tensor construction pipeline that facilitates end-to-end spatiotemporal learning with a video transformer, eliminating hand-crafted feature engineering; (3) a systematic multi-task evaluation with LOSO validation across four cognitive paradigms; and (4) statistical evidence grounding the selection of HbT over HbO and HbR as the primary hemodynamic feature for GAD classification.
