Recent research using functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) consistently shows hypoactivation in the prefrontal cortex (PFC), particularly involving reduced oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO) and altered deoxygenated hemoglobin (HbR) responses, in individuals with generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) compared to healthy controls during cognitive tasks like verbal fluency tests (VFT).[1] Activation patterns in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) and orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) often reveal diagnostic utility, with GAD exhibiting less severe deficits than comorbid or other anxiety-related conditions, though no top-tier (e.g., Nature, Science) publications dominate this niche; instead, high-impact specialized journals like Frontiers in Psychiatry and Asian Journal of Psychiatry feature recent (2024-2026) studies from established Chinese clinical research groups.[1] These findings highlight fNIRS as a promising, motion-tolerant tool for probing hemodynamic responses in anxiety, with implications for adjunctive diagnostics amid limitations like medication confounds and small samples.[4][1]

### 1. fNIRS in GAD, MDD, and Comorbidity (2025, Largest Cohort)
Shen et al. (2025) compared PFC hemodynamic responses via fNIRS-VFT in 75 GAD patients, 75 major depressive disorder (MDD) patients, 71 with MDD-GAD comorbidity (CMG), and 75 healthy controls (HCs). Key findings: All patient groups showed significantly lower HbO activation in PFC vs. HCs (Kruskal-Wallis p<0.05); GAD had higher PFC activation than MDD (especially right OFC) and CMG (left ventrolateral PFC). Deep learning classification achieved 60-77% accuracy, with ROC-AUC up to 0.86 (left frontopolar PFC for MDD). Methodology used 52-channel fNIRS focusing on HbO integrals; implications include fNIRS-VFT as an adjunct diagnostic for differentiating GAD from MDD/CMG (venue: Asian J Psychiatry, Tier 3, DOI:10.1016/j.ajp.2025.104382).[1]

### 2. OCD vs. GAD Brain Functioning (2026, Direct DLPFC Comparison)
Wang et al. (2026) examined 31 GAD, 31 OCD, and 31 HCs using 53-channel fNIRS during VFT, targeting DLPFC, frontopolar area (FPA), Broca's area, etc.[1] GAD and OCD both showed HbO hypoactivation vs. HCs (e.g., bilateral FPA, Broca's; ANOVA p<0.05), but OCD had more severe left DLPFC deficits (integral/mean values lower, post-hoc p<0.05). Left Broca's HbO negatively correlated with OCD compulsions (r=-0.45 to -0.56, p<0.05); right FPA integral discriminated patients from HCs (AUC=0.769).[1] This highlights shared PFC hypoactivation as transdiagnostic, with OCD-specific DLPFC severity (Front. Psychiatry, Tier 3, DOI:10.3389/fpsyt.2026.1757526; authors from Sichuan Provincial Mental Health Center).[1]

### 3. GAD vs. GAD+ADHD Comorbidity (2024)
A 2024 study (J Psychiatr Res) used fNIRS GO/NOGO and VFT in 49 GAD+ADHD, 46 GAD, and 34 HCs, finding reduced HbO in medial/left ventrolateral PFC across GAD groups vs. HCs, but left DLPFC activation during GO/NOGO correlated with ADHD inattention and distinguished comorbidities. No OFC-specific data, but supports PFC hypoactivation patterns in GAD (DOI:10.1016/j.jpsychires.2024.02.053).

### 4. Anxious Depression DLPFC Patterns (2024)
In 194 depressed patients (103 anxious, 91 non-anxious) and 110 HCs, fNIRS during emotional autobiographical memory task showed higher left DLPFC HbO under negative valence in anxious depression vs. non-anxious (no valence difference in anxious group), suggesting hyperactivation linked to anxiety overlay.[10] Implications for GAD-like anxious states (PubMed PMID:39019227).[10]

## PFC Subregion Patterns

### DLPFC Activation
DLPFC hypoactivation is prominent in GAD during inhibitory/cognitive tasks, mediating anxiety-inhibitory deficits (e.g., serial mediation in anxiety-sleep links).[6] In GAD vs. OCD, left DLPFC integrals were higher in GAD (p<0.05), indicating less impairment.[1] HbO reductions correlate negatively with anxiety severity (r=-0.2 to -0.6).[6]

### OFC and Orbitofrontal Patterns
Right OFC showed highest 4-group classification accuracy (60%) in Shen et al., with CMG > GAD activation. Sparse direct GAD-OFC data; one review notes OFC in implicit emotion regulation deficits shared with MDD (reduced activation). fNIRS sensitivity to OFC is moderate due to depth limits (~2-3cm).

### HbO vs. HbR Responses
HbO increases are reliably lower in GAD PFC (e.g., VFT integrals), with HbR showing smaller, variable reductions; HbO preferred for SNR.[1] No GAD-specific HbR controversies noted.

## Methodologies and Limitations
Most studies (2024-2026) use 48-53 channel fNIRS (e.g., BS-5000L), VFT/GO/NOGO tasks (30-60s blocks), HOMER2 processing (MBLL, baseline correction), and ROI analyses (Brodmann-based).[1] Limitations: Medication confounds (SSRIs alter neurovascular coupling), small samples (n=30-75/group), no Tier-1 venues, cross-sectional designs preventing causality; conflicting valence effects (hypo- vs. hyper-activation).[1][10] No major controversies, but replication needed for diagnostics (AUCs 0.65-0.86 exploratory).

## Research Gaps and Future Directions
Gaps include pure GAD cohorts without comorbidity, OFC/DLPFC connectivity (resting-state fNIRS), longitudinal pre/post-treatment HbO/HbR, and top-tier validations. Future: Integrate fNIRS with EEG for deeper OFC, test in diverse populations, target DLPFC rTMS guided by baselines.[1]
